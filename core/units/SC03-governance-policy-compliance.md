## Overview

This unit teaches the governance discipline that turns security from a collection
of controls into a managed, accountable program. It covers governance structures
and the **Govern** function of **NIST CSF 2.0**, the policy hierarchy (policy →
standard → procedure → guideline), and the major compliance regimes an Australian
organisation must navigate — **ISO/IEC 27001:2022**, **SOC 2**, **APRA CPS 234**,
the **SOCI Act**, and **IRAP/ISM** for government work. The central, deliberately
commercial thesis of the unit is that **compliance is a revenue and market-access
enabler, not merely a cost**: the right certification opens the right doors, and
the practitioner's job is to choose and run the regime that fits the industry and
unlocks the most value.

Governance is where the degree's strategic thread is most explicit. It draws on
SC01 (risk) and SC02 (architecture) and sets up SC04 (vendor risk), SC05 (program
management), and SC06 (stakeholder communication), as well as the GRC and
Leadership majors. In the Australian context it is grounded in OAIC privacy
obligations, APRA prudential standards, the SOCI Act, and the ACSC ISM.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

1. **Explain** security governance structures and the NIST CSF 2.0 Govern
   function.
2. **Apply** the policy hierarchy to draft fit-for-purpose security policies and
   standards.
3. **Differentiate** the major compliance regimes (ISO 27001, SOC 2, APRA CPS 234,
   SOCI, IRAP/ISM) and match each to industry and business goal.
4. **Apply** a compliance-mapping approach to show how one control set can satisfy
   multiple regimes.
5. **Analyse** compliance as a commercial enabler and build the business case for a
   certification.
6. **Examine** assurance and audit processes and how an organisation prepares for
   and sustains certification.

## Topics

### Topic 1: Security Governance and NIST CSF 2.0 Govern

Governance is how an organisation directs, accountable, and oversees its security.
This topic covers governance structures (board oversight, executive
accountability, security steering, the role of a CISO), the **Govern** function
introduced in NIST CSF 2.0, and how governance connects strategy, risk, and
operations.

**Key concepts:**
- Governance structures and accountability
- NIST CSF 2.0 Govern function
- Linking strategy, risk, and operations

---

### Topic 2: The Policy Hierarchy

Policy is governance written down. This topic teaches the hierarchy — **policy**
(intent and authority), **standard** (mandatory specifics), **procedure**
(step-by-step), **guideline** (recommended practice) — and the lifecycle of
drafting, approval, communication, exception handling, and review. Students learn
to write policy that is enforceable and usable, not shelfware.

**Key concepts:**
- Policy / standard / procedure / guideline
- Policy lifecycle and exception management
- Writing enforceable, usable policy

---

### Topic 3: The Compliance Landscape

This topic maps the regimes a practitioner must know. **ISO/IEC 27001:2022** — a
certifiable ISMS, broadly recognised. **SOC 2** — trust-services attestation
prized by enterprise SaaS buyers. **APRA CPS 234** — mandatory for APRA-regulated
finance. **SOCI Act** — critical-infrastructure obligations. **IRAP/ISM** — the
gateway to Australian government work. Students learn the purpose, audience, and
shape of each.

**Key concepts:**
- ISO 27001, SOC 2, APRA CPS 234, SOCI, IRAP/ISM
- Certification vs attestation vs regulatory obligation
- Audience and recognition of each regime

**Australian context:** APRA CPS 234, SOCI, and IRAP/ISM are distinctly
Australian; ISO 27001 and SOC 2 are globally recognised but heavily used here.

---

### Topic 4: Compliance Mapping and Control Rationalisation

Organisations rarely face one regime. This topic teaches mapping a single control
set to multiple frameworks so that one well-run control satisfies many
requirements — reducing duplication and audit fatigue. It introduces crosswalks
(e.g. ISO 27001 ↔ NIST CSF ↔ Essential Eight ↔ SOC 2 criteria) and the idea of a
unified control framework.

**Key concepts:**
- Control crosswalks across regimes
- Unified control frameworks and "test once, satisfy many"
- Reducing duplication and audit burden

---

### Topic 5: Compliance as a Commercial Enabler

This is the unit's signature topic. Compliance is reframed as a growth lever:
**SOC 2** unlocks enterprise SaaS deals; **IRAP** unlocks government contracts;
**APRA CPS 234** and **ISO 27001** unlock regulated and international markets;
demonstrable compliance shortens sales cycles and builds customer trust. Students
learn to select the regime that opens the most valuable doors for a given industry
and to quantify the commercial upside, not just the cost.

**Key concepts:**
- Compliance as market access and trust
- Matching regime to industry and revenue goal
- Quantifying the commercial upside of certification

**Australian context:** IRAP for government, APRA/ISO for finance, SOC 2 for SaaS
selling into Australian enterprise — mapped explicitly to local markets.

---

### Topic 6: Assurance, Audit, and Sustaining Compliance

Certification is earned and kept. This topic covers the assurance lifecycle:
readiness assessment, evidence collection, internal audit, the external audit/
attestation process, addressing findings, and continuous compliance (rather than
annual scramble). It connects evidence to the architecture traceability (SC02) and
risk register (SC01).

**Key concepts:**
- Readiness, evidence, and internal audit
- The external audit/attestation process
- Continuous compliance and avoiding the annual scramble

## Labs & Exercises

### Lab 1: Policy & Governance Drafting

**Objective:** Draft a fit-for-purpose security policy and supporting standard
within a defined governance structure.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 1 and 2

**Environment:**
- Operating System: any
- Tools: a document editor, NIST CSF 2.0 and ISO 27001 Annex A references (free)
- Minimum hardware: trivial

**Instructions:**

1. Take a scenario organisation and define a minimal governance structure (who
   owns, approves, and oversees security).
2. Draft one security policy (e.g. Access Control Policy) with purpose, scope,
   authority, and policy statements.
3. Draft one supporting standard with mandatory specifics (e.g. MFA and password
   requirements).
4. Add an exception process and a review cadence.
5. Map the policy/standard to the relevant NIST CSF 2.0 outcomes and ISO 27001
   Annex A controls.
6. Note how this artefact would serve an audit.

**Expected Output:**

A complete policy plus supporting standard, with governance ownership, exception
handling, review cadence, and framework mapping. Learners can explain why the
policy is enforceable and audit-ready.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. Where is the line between policy and standard, and why does it matter?
2. How would this policy be evidenced in a SOC 2 or ISO 27001 audit?
3. What makes a policy "shelfware", and how did you avoid it?

---

### Lab 2: Multi-Framework Compliance Mapping & Business Case

**Objective:** Map one control set across multiple regimes and build the commercial
case for a chosen certification.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 3–6 and Lab 1

**Environment:**
- Operating System: any
- Tools: a spreadsheet, framework references (NIST CSF, ISO 27001, SOC 2 criteria,
  Essential Eight)
- Minimum hardware: trivial

**Instructions:**

1. Take a scenario organisation and its industry (e.g. a SaaS firm selling to
   Australian banks).
2. Select the most commercially valuable regime(s) for that industry and justify
   the choice.
3. Build a crosswalk: take 10 controls and map each to NIST CSF, ISO 27001 Annex A,
   SOC 2 criteria, and the Essential Eight where applicable.
4. Identify where one control satisfies multiple regimes ("test once, satisfy
   many").
5. Build a one-page business case: the markets/contracts the certification unlocks,
   indicative cost, and expected commercial upside.
6. Outline a readiness roadmap to certification.

**Expected Output:**

A multi-framework crosswalk showing control rationalisation, a justified regime
selection, a commercial business case, and a readiness roadmap. Learners can argue
the certification as an investment with a return.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. How much duplication did control rationalisation remove?
2. For a different industry (government vs fintech vs health), how would the regime
   choice change?
3. What is the strongest commercial argument for the certification you chose?

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment: Regime-to-Industry Matching

**Type:** Short-answer exercise with answer key

**Description:** Given organisations across industries, students select the
compliance regime(s) that best fit and explain the commercial rationale.
Self-marked.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO3, LO5

**Feedback mechanism:** Answer key with recommended regimes and commercial
rationale per organisation.

---

### Summative Assessment: Governance & Compliance Strategy

**Type:** Strategy report

**Description:** For an assigned Australian organisation, students (a) propose a
governance structure and a key policy/standard, (b) select and justify the
compliance regime(s) for its industry, (c) produce a multi-framework control
crosswalk demonstrating rationalisation, (d) build the commercial business case for
certification, and (e) outline a readiness and continuous-compliance roadmap.
Deliverable: 2,500–3,000 word report with policy artefact, crosswalk, and business
case.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO1, LO2, LO3, LO4, LO5, LO6

**Assessment-Learning Outcome Mapping:**

| Assessment Task | Learning Outcomes |
|---|---|
| Governance & policy | LO1, LO2 |
| Regime selection | LO3 |
| Control crosswalk | LO4 |
| Commercial case & roadmap | LO5, LO6 |

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Governance & policy | Clear structure; enforceable, audit-ready policy | Sound governance and policy | Partial or vague | Weak or unusable |
| Compliance knowledge | Precise, industry-fit regime selection | Mostly correct selection | Generic selection | Incorrect selection |
| Control rationalisation | Insightful crosswalk; real duplication removed | Solid crosswalk | Partial crosswalk | Little rationalisation |
| Commercial framing | Compelling, quantified business case | Reasonable case | Superficial case | No commercial case |
| Communication | Executive-ready, clear | Clear with minor lapses | Disorganised | Unclear |

## Australian Context

This unit incorporates the following Australian context:

- **APRA CPS 234:** Mandatory information-security regime for regulated finance.
- **SOCI Act:** Critical-infrastructure governance and obligations.
- **IRAP / Australian Government ISM:** The gateway to Australian government work,
  framed as a commercial enabler.
- **OAIC / Privacy Act:** Governance obligations for personal information.
