## Overview

Technical excellence is wasted if it cannot be communicated to the people who fund,
govern, and act on it. This unit teaches students to translate cybersecurity into
the language of its audiences — boards and executives, technical teams, customers,
regulators, and the public — across both routine reporting and crisis. It covers
audience analysis, risk and security reporting, board-level communication, crisis
and incident communication (including Australian breach-notification obligations),
and the influencing skills that turn a security case into a funded decision.

Communication is the capstone of the Strategic Core: the risk (SC01), architecture
(SC02), governance/compliance (SC03), vendor (SC04), and program (SC05) work only
creates value when it is understood and acted on. The unit reinforces the degree's
commercial thread — security communicated well is what secures budget, wins
customer trust, and turns compliance into a selling point. In the Australian
context it integrates OAIC, ACSC, and APRA notification and reporting expectations.
SC06 supports the Leadership, GRC, and DFIR majors.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

1. **Analyse** a stakeholder audience and adapt security messaging to its needs and
   level.
2. **Apply** risk and security reporting techniques to communicate posture to
   executives and boards.
3. **Apply** crisis and incident communication practices, including Australian
   notification obligations.
4. **Demonstrate** translating technical findings into business-relevant language
   and decisions.
5. **Examine** influencing and persuasion techniques to secure support and funding
   for security.
6. **Analyse** the communication dimension of compliance as a trust and commercial
   enabler.

## Topics

### Topic 1: Audience Analysis and Message Framing

The same fact must be told differently to different audiences. This topic teaches
analysing stakeholders (their concerns, knowledge, and decisions), and framing
messages accordingly — risk and value for executives, specifics for technical
teams, reassurance and obligation for customers and regulators. It introduces the
discipline of leading with the "so what".

**Key concepts:**
- Stakeholder analysis: concerns, knowledge, decisions
- Framing by audience; leading with impact
- Avoiding jargon without losing accuracy

---

### Topic 2: Risk and Security Reporting

Reporting is the routine heartbeat of security communication. This topic covers
designing reports and dashboards for different levels, presenting risk (from SC01)
and metrics (from SC05) clearly, using visualisation honestly, and tailoring cadence
and depth. It stresses reports that drive decisions rather than describe activity.

**Key concepts:**
- Layered reporting (operational → executive → board)
- Honest visualisation of risk and metrics
- Decision-oriented reporting

---

### Topic 3: Communicating with the Board and Executives

Boards govern risk and allocate capital; they need security in those terms. This
topic teaches board-level communication: tying security to business risk and
strategy, expressing risk appetite, answering "are we secure / are we spending
enough", and earning credibility. It links to NIST CSF 2.0 Govern oversight
expectations.

**Key concepts:**
- Security as business risk and strategy at board level
- Answering the board's real questions
- Building credibility and trust over time

---

### Topic 4: Crisis and Incident Communication

In a crisis, communication failures compound technical ones. This topic covers
incident communication: internal coordination, executive and board updates,
customer and public messaging, and — central in Australia — the **NDB** breach
notifications to the OAIC and individuals, **SOCI** reporting to the ACSC, and APRA
notification for regulated entities. It addresses tone, timing, accuracy, and
legal review.

**Key concepts:**
- Internal and external crisis communication
- Australian notification obligations (NDB, SOCI, APRA)
- Tone, timing, accuracy, and legal sign-off

**Australian context:** Built around Australian breach-notification obligations.

---

### Topic 5: Influencing and Securing Support

Security leaders rarely have direct authority over everything they need. This topic
covers influence and persuasion: building coalitions, the business case as a
persuasion tool (linking to SC05's budget case), storytelling with data, and
negotiating priorities. It frames communication as the means by which security gets
resourced and adopted.

**Key concepts:**
- Influence without authority; building coalitions
- Persuasion with evidence and narrative
- Negotiating priorities and securing funding

---

### Topic 6: Communication as a Trust and Commercial Enabler

This topic closes the Strategic Core. Well-communicated security and compliance
build trust that has commercial value — customers buy from vendors they trust, and
a clear security story (backed by SOC 2/ISO/IRAP) shortens sales cycles and
differentiates. Conversely, poor breach communication destroys trust and value.
Students learn to treat communication as part of the commercial value of security.

**Key concepts:**
- Trust as a commercial asset
- Communicating compliance/certification as a differentiator
- The reputational cost of poor communication

**Australian context:** Australian breach cases illustrate how communication shapes
trust and commercial outcomes.

## Labs & Exercises

### Lab 1: Board-Level Security Briefing

**Objective:** Translate a technical security posture into a board-level briefing
that drives a funding decision.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 1–3 and 5

**Environment:**
- Operating System: any
- Tools: presentation/document software, the SC01 risk and SC05 program/metrics
  artefacts (or provided equivalents)
- Minimum hardware: trivial

**Instructions:**

1. Take a provided technical security posture (risks, maturity, incidents, program
   needs).
2. Identify the board's likely concerns and the decision you want from them.
3. Produce a concise board briefing (max ~6 slides or 2 pages) leading with
   business risk and the decision sought.
4. Express the security posture in risk/value terms, not technical detail.
5. Include one clear ask (e.g. fund an initiative) with its risk and commercial
   justification.
6. Anticipate three tough board questions and prepare answers.

**Expected Output:**

A board-ready briefing leading with the decision, posture in business terms, a
justified ask, and prepared answers to tough questions. Learners can deliver the
"so what" in one sentence.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. What did you cut to make it board-appropriate, and why?
2. How did you express technical risk without technical language?
3. Which justification — risk or commercial — led your ask, and why?

---

### Lab 2: Crisis Communication & Notification

**Objective:** Produce a coordinated set of crisis communications for a breach,
including Australian regulatory notifications.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 4 and 6

**Environment:**
- Operating System: any
- Tools: document templates, OAIC NDB and ACSC/APRA notification references (free)
- Minimum hardware: trivial

**Instructions:**

1. Take a breach scenario (e.g. customer data exposed at an Australian company).
2. Draft an internal executive/board update for the first 24 hours.
3. Draft a customer notification appropriate to the NDB scheme's required content.
4. Draft the statement to the OAIC and note SOCI/APRA notifications if the entity is
   in scope, with timeframes.
5. Draft a brief public/holding statement.
6. Annotate each communication with its timing and required legal/executive sign-off.

**Expected Output:**

A coordinated communication set (internal, customer, regulator, public) meeting NDB
content requirements, with timing and sign-off noted. Learners can explain how the
messages stay consistent and accurate across audiences.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. How did the message change across audiences while staying truthful and
   consistent?
2. How did the NDB/SOCI/APRA obligations constrain timing and content?
3. How does communication in this crisis protect (or destroy) commercial trust?

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment: Translate the Tech

**Type:** Short-exercise with answer key

**Description:** Given several technical findings/statements, students rewrite each
for a board audience — accurate, jargon-free, and leading with impact. Self-marked.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO1, LO4

**Feedback mechanism:** Answer key with a model board-level rewrite for each item.

---

### Summative Assessment: Communication Portfolio

**Type:** Practical portfolio

**Description:** For an assigned Australian organisation, students produce a
communication portfolio: (a) a board-level security briefing with a funding ask, (b)
a layered reporting design (operational→board), (c) a full crisis-communication set
for a breach including NDB/SOCI/APRA notifications, and (d) a short reflection on how
communication functions as a trust and commercial enabler. Deliverable: a portfolio
(~2,500–3,000 words equivalent across artefacts).

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO1, LO2, LO3, LO4, LO5, LO6

**Assessment-Learning Outcome Mapping:**

| Assessment Task | Learning Outcomes |
|---|---|
| Board briefing & funding ask | LO2, LO3, LO5 |
| Layered reporting design | LO1, LO2 |
| Crisis communication set | LO3, LO4 |
| Trust/commercial reflection | LO6 |

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Audience adaptation | Precisely pitched to each audience | Mostly well-pitched | Partial adaptation | Poorly pitched |
| Reporting & board comms | Clear, decision-driving, credible | Clear and sound | Descriptive, not decisive | Unclear |
| Crisis communication | Coordinated, compliant, accurate, well-timed | Sound with minor gaps | Partial or inconsistent | Non-compliant or confused |
| Influence & commercial framing | Persuasive; trust/commercial value clear | Reasonable persuasion | Superficial | Unpersuasive |
| Communication quality | Polished, professional | Clear with minor lapses | Disorganised | Unclear |

  Lab 2.

  for in-scope entities.
- **Australian breach cases:** Used to illustrate how communication shapes trust and
  commercial outcomes.
