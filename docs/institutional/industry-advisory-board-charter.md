# Industry Advisory Board — Terms of Reference

## 1. Purpose

The Industry Advisory Board (IAB) provides strategic oversight of the degree's
alignment to industry needs, workforce trends, and the Australian threat landscape.
The IAB ensures the degree remains credible, current, and relevant to employers
and practitioners.

The IAB is distinct from the governance roles in `docs/governance.md`, which handle
day-to-day content review. The IAB operates at the program level.

---

## 2. Functions

The IAB:

1. **Reviews program-level curriculum** annually, providing advice on whether the
   degree's structure, majors, and progression pathway reflect industry needs
2. **Monitors workforce trends** and alerts the Maintainer group when significant
   changes in the cybersecurity workforce require curriculum response
3. **Validates the framework alignment** of the degree at the program level —
   confirming that the NICE DCWF, SFIA, and ASD mappings reflect the skills
   employers actually use
4. **Provides employer endorsement** — IAB members may individually endorse the
   degree for their organisation's hiring and development purposes
5. **Connects the project to industry** — introduces potential Domain Experts,
   Practitioner Reviewers, and community contributors from their networks
6. **Advises on the accreditation pathway** — provides input on the feasibility and
   priority of pursuing formal TEQSA recognition or industry body endorsement
7. **Champions the degree** in the Australian cybersecurity community — at conferences,
   in employer forums, and through professional networks

---

## 3. Membership

### Composition

The IAB comprises 7–12 members. Composition should reflect:
- Both operational and strategic cybersecurity disciplines
- Australian government/defence, financial services, and commercial sectors
- At least one academic from an Australian university
- At least one representative from an Australian professional body (AISA, ACS, or equivalent)
- Geographic diversity across Australian states and territories where possible

### Member Criteria

IAB members must:
- Be currently active in the Australian cybersecurity industry or academia
- Have at least 5 years of cybersecurity experience
- Be willing to commit to the time requirements below
- Disclose any vendor or commercial conflicts of interest

### Time Commitment

- Two IAB meetings per year (2 hours each, online or hybrid)
- Annual curriculum review (2–3 hours, asynchronous)
- Ad-hoc advice as requested (estimated 2–3 hours per year total)

**Total estimated commitment: 8–10 hours per year**

### Appointment

- IAB members are appointed by the Maintainer group
- Terms are 2 years, renewable
- Members may resign at any time with 30 days notice
- The Maintainer group may remove a member for persistent non-participation,
  undisclosed conflict of interest, or conduct inconsistent with the code of conduct

### Remuneration

IAB members serve in a voluntary capacity. There is no remuneration.
The project will publicly acknowledge IAB members (with their consent) in
the repository and any public communications.

---

## 4. Governance Relationship

The IAB advises — it does not govern. Final decisions on curriculum, governance,
and content remain with the Maintainer group.

The IAB does not:
- Have merge authority over pull requests
- Override Domain Expert or Practitioner Reviewer decisions on unit content
- Set governance rules (this is the Maintainer group's authority)

The IAB does:
- Formally advise on program structure and major relevance
- Provide a formal record of industry input (important for TEQSA)
- Escalate significant curriculum concerns to the Maintainer group for decision

---

## 5. Meeting Structure

### IAB Meeting Agenda (Standard)

1. Confirming quorum and conflicts of interest (5 min)
2. Updates from Maintainer group — curriculum progress, published units, significant changes (15 min)
3. Industry update — members share relevant workforce, regulatory, or threat landscape developments (20 min)
4. Curriculum review item — one specific area of the curriculum tabled for advisory input (30 min)
5. Framework currency check — any new framework versions requiring action (10 min)
6. Emerging issues — open discussion (15 min)
7. Action items and next meeting (5 min)

### Quorum

A minimum of 5 IAB members must be present for formal advice to be recorded.

### Records

Meeting minutes are recorded and published in the repository (with member consent)
at `docs/quality/iad-meeting-records/` (to be created when the IAB is constituted).

---

## 6. Conflicts of Interest

IAB members must disclose:
- Employer relationships with tool vendors referenced in the curriculum
- Financial relationships with certification bodies
- Employment at institutions that may compete with or partner with this degree
- Any personal financial interest in the degree's success

Disclosed conflicts are noted in meeting minutes. Members with a conflict on a
specific agenda item should abstain from advising on that item.

---

## 7. Establishing the IAB

The IAB is a planned governance element. It has not yet been constituted.

**To establish the IAB, the Maintainer group should:**
1. Identify 7–10 potential founding members from the Australian cybersecurity community
2. Approach candidates individually with this charter and an invitation
3. Convene a founding meeting to confirm membership and elect a Chair
4. Record the IAB constitution in this document with the list of founding members

**Criteria for founding the IAB:**
The IAB should be constituted when:
- At least 6 Foundation units are Published
- At least 2 Maintainers are active
- The project has demonstrable community engagement (GitHub stars, contributors, etc.)
