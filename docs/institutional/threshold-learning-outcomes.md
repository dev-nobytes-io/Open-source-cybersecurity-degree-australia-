# Threshold Learning Outcomes

Threshold Learning Outcomes (TLOs) define the minimum standard a student must
demonstrate to be awarded the degree. They represent the floor of graduate
capability — every student who completes this degree should be able to demonstrate
all TLOs regardless of their chosen major.

TLOs are required for TEQSA accreditation and for formal credit recognition
discussions with other institutions. They are distinct from learning outcomes
within individual units, which may exceed TLO requirements.

These TLOs are aligned to the Australian Cybersecurity TLO framework (where
applicable) and to AQF Level 7 graduate capabilities.

---

## TLO 1 — Security Fundamentals

**Statement:** Graduates can explain and apply core cybersecurity principles —
including confidentiality, integrity, availability, defence-in-depth, and
least privilege — to realistic technical and organisational scenarios.

**Minimum evidence:**
- Can classify threats using a recognised threat model (e.g., STRIDE, ATT&CK)
- Can identify and describe the security implications of common technical configurations
- Can apply the CIA triad to analyse a given scenario and recommend controls

**Assessed in:** F04, OC01 (or SC01)

---

## TLO 2 — Australian Legal and Regulatory Literacy

**Statement:** Graduates understand the Australian legal and regulatory framework
governing cybersecurity, including key legislation and the obligations it creates
for organisations and practitioners.

**Minimum evidence:**
- Can identify the obligations created by the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and the NDB scheme
- Can describe the scope of the Security of Critical Infrastructure Act 2018 and
  identify which sectors it covers
- Can explain a practitioner's legal obligations when handling digital evidence
  under the Australian Evidence Act 1995

**Assessed in:** F05, and the Australian context component of major units

---

## TLO 3 — Technical Proficiency

**Statement:** Graduates can operate the technical tools and environments relevant
to their chosen pathway at a practitioner level, and can apply those tools to solve
real-world problems in controlled environments.

**Minimum evidence (Operations):**
- Can capture, filter, and interpret network traffic using standard analysis tools
- Can operate a Linux command line at an intermediate level
- Can write a basic script in Python or Bash to automate a security task
- Can query a SIEM or log analysis platform to identify security events

**Minimum evidence (Strategy):**
- Can use scripting tools to automate a governance or risk data task
- Can interpret network and system logs sufficient to understand a security event
- Can use a GRC or risk management tool to document a risk register

**Assessed in:** F01, F02, F03, F06 (all students); respective Core and Major units

---

## TLO 4 — Framework Mapping

**Statement:** Graduates can map their professional activities to at least two
recognised cybersecurity workforce frameworks, including at least one Australian
framework, and can articulate their competencies in terms employers understand.

**Minimum evidence:**
- Can identify the NICE DCWF work role(s) most relevant to their specialisation
  and explain which tasks they have demonstrated
- Can describe their skills using SFIA skill codes and responsibility levels
  appropriate to their experience
- Can map their work to at least one ASD Cyber Skills Framework domain

**Assessed in:** Throughout — demonstrated via the competency profile
(`templates/competency-profile-template.md`) and unit framework mapping sections

---

## TLO 5 — Analytical Reasoning

**Statement:** Graduates can gather, evaluate, and synthesise information from
multiple sources to produce a reasoned cybersecurity analysis, recommendation,
or decision — with appropriate acknowledgement of uncertainty and limitations.

**Minimum evidence:**
- Can produce a written analysis that distinguishes fact from inference
- Can assess the confidence and reliability of a data source or intelligence item
- Can identify gaps in available evidence and state what additional information
  would be required to increase confidence in a finding

**Assessed in:** Core units (OC01, SC01, and equivalents); major assessment tasks

---

## TLO 6 — Ethical and Professional Conduct

**Statement:** Graduates demonstrate ethical reasoning and professional conduct
appropriate to the cybersecurity discipline, including awareness of legal constraints,
authorisation requirements, and the impact of cybersecurity work on individuals
and organisations.

**Minimum evidence:**
- Can articulate the authorisation requirements for offensive security testing
  and explain the legal consequences of unauthorised access under Australian law
- Can apply a professional ethics framework to a scenario involving a conflict
  between employer instructions and practitioner obligations
- Can describe the privacy obligations applicable to handling personal data
  encountered during a security engagement

**Assessed in:** F05; CTE ethical framework units; GRC and Leadership major units

---

## TLO 7 — Communication

**Statement:** Graduates can communicate cybersecurity findings, risk, and
recommendations clearly and accurately in writing, calibrated to a defined audience.

**Minimum evidence:**
- Can write a technical report of their own lab work that is accurate, structured,
  and readable by a peer practitioner
- Can write an executive summary of a technical finding that is comprehensible
  to a non-technical manager
- Can produce a risk statement that links a technical finding to a business impact

**Assessed in:** Summative assessments across all layers; capstone project

---

## TLO 8 — Continuous Learning Disposition

**Statement:** Graduates demonstrate awareness that cybersecurity is a rapidly
evolving field and can identify strategies and resources for maintaining professional
currency throughout their career.

**Minimum evidence:**
- Can identify and describe at least three sources they would use to stay current
  with the Australian threat landscape (e.g., ACSC advisories, ASD publications,
  AISA resources)
- Can explain how a specific framework (NICE DCWF, ATT&CK, SFIA) is versioned and
  how they would track changes
- Can describe their approach to maintaining and updating their competency profile
  as their skills develop

**Assessed in:** Further reading sections across units; capstone reflection component

## Pathway-Specific Additional TLOs

### Operations Pathway TLO

**TLO 9 — Operational Execution**

Graduates can plan and execute a technical cybersecurity operation (threat hunting
exercise, incident response, forensic investigation, or equivalent) in a controlled
environment, producing documented outputs appropriate to professional standards.

**Assessed in:** Major capstone project

### Strategy Pathway TLO

**TLO 9 — Strategic Communication and Leadership**

Graduates can design a cybersecurity program component (risk framework, compliance
assessment, security architecture, or leadership strategy) and present it to a
simulated executive or board audience with appropriate framing for business impact.

**Assessed in:** Major capstone project
