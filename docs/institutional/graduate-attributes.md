# Graduate Attributes

This document describes the attributes of a graduate of the Open-Source Cybersecurity
Degree. Graduate attributes go beyond specific technical skills to describe the
capabilities, dispositions, and professional identity that the degree develops.

Graduate attributes are required for TEQSA HESF compliance (Domain 3: Teaching)
and are used by professional bodies, employers, and credit recognition partners
to assess the calibre of the qualification.

---

## Overview

A graduate of this degree is a cybersecurity practitioner who:

1. **Thinks in frameworks** — instinctively maps problems to recognised standards
   and can articulate their thinking in terms employers understand
2. **Acts with Australian context** — understands the regulatory, legislative, and
   threat environment specific to Australian organisations
3. **Reasons ethically** — applies legal, professional, and ethical judgement to
   every technical decision
4. **Communicates with precision** — can explain complex technical findings to
   technical and non-technical audiences
5. **Pursues currency** — treats learning as a continuous professional obligation,
   not a one-time event
6. **Builds with evidence** — makes decisions backed by data, logs, intelligence,
   and documented reasoning — not intuition alone

---

## Attribute 1 — Framework Fluency

**Description:** Graduates apply recognised cybersecurity frameworks — NIST CSF 2.0,
MITRE ATT&CK, ASD Essential Eight, ISO 27001, and others — as natural tools of
professional practice, not as compliance checklists.

**What this looks like:**
- Mapping a threat actor's behaviour to specific ATT&CK techniques without prompting
- Describing a security gap using NIST CSF functions and categories
- Selecting the right framework for a given context (risk management vs. threat
  detection vs. governance) rather than applying a single framework to every problem

**How the degree develops this:** Every unit requires explicit framework mapping.
Students apply frameworks in labs and assessments, not just study them in theory.

---

## Attribute 2 — Australian Professional Context

**Description:** Graduates understand and operate within the Australian regulatory,
legislative, and threat environment. They do not apply generic international
frameworks without understanding Australian variations and obligations.

**What this looks like:**
- Citing the Privacy Act 1988 and NDB scheme obligations when advising on a
  data breach response — not just GDPR
- Recognising SOCI Act obligations when scoping work for a critical infrastructure operator
- Awareness of ASD advisories, the ACSC's threat landscape reporting, and IRAP processes
- Understanding APRA CPS 234 implications for financial sector clients

**How the degree develops this:** Every unit requires at least one Australian context
element (legislation, regulator, case study, or framework). F05 and GRC units build
deep regulatory literacy.

---

## Attribute 3 — Ethical and Legal Reasoning

**Description:** Graduates apply rigorous ethical and legal reasoning to all
cybersecurity work, including offensive operations, data handling, and professional conduct.

**What this looks like:**
- Refusing or pushing back on work that lacks proper authorisation
- Understanding the legal basis for digital forensic evidence admissibility
  under the Australian Evidence Act 1995
- Applying responsible disclosure principles when vulnerabilities are discovered
- Treating personal data encountered in investigations with appropriate care
  under the Privacy Act 1988

**How the degree develops this:** F05 (Legal, Ethics & Australian Compliance) is
a Foundation unit completed by all students. The CTE major includes a dedicated
ethical and legal framework. All operational units include Australian legal context.

---

## Attribute 4 — Technical Communication

**Description:** Graduates communicate technical findings, risk, and recommendations
clearly and accurately to both technical and non-technical audiences.

**What this looks like:**
- Writing an incident report that is useful to both the responding SOC analyst
  and the executive stakeholder
- Presenting threat intelligence in a format calibrated to the audience's role
  (tactical, operational, or strategic intelligence)
- Translating a risk finding into business impact language for a board presentation

**How the degree develops this:** Every summative assessment requires written
communication. SC06 (Stakeholder Communication) and the Leadership major develop
executive communication skills. LD and GRC capstones include board reporting.

---

## Attribute 5 — Evidence-Based Practice

**Description:** Graduates make professional decisions based on documented evidence,
structured analysis, and transparent reasoning — not assumption or intuition.

**What this looks like:**
- Conducting threat hunting with documented hypotheses and evidence chains
- Writing forensic findings that include chain-of-custody documentation
- Producing intelligence assessments with explicit confidence ratings and source attribution
- Making risk recommendations with documented threat data and control effectiveness evidence

**How the degree develops this:** Lab-first design means students practice evidence
collection and documentation in every unit. Assessment rubrics reward well-evidenced
reasoning over correct conclusions reached by guesswork.

---

## Attribute 6 — Professional Accountability

**Description:** Graduates take responsibility for the quality and accuracy of their
work, acknowledge limitations and uncertainty, and engage in continuous professional
development.

**What this looks like:**
- Accurately representing their skill level and experience to employers
- Acknowledging when a situation is outside their competency and escalating appropriately
- Maintaining and updating their competency profile as skills develop
- Engaging with the practitioner community and contributing to knowledge sharing

**How the degree develops this:** The competency portfolio system (NICE DCWF T-code
evidence) teaches self-assessment discipline. The degree's emphasis on accuracy over
speed (Rule R5) models the professional standard. The capstone requires honest
self-evaluation of project outcomes.

---

## Attribute 7 — Adaptive Problem-Solving

**Description:** Graduates approach novel or complex cybersecurity problems
systematically, drawing on a range of frameworks and techniques rather than
applying memorised solutions.

**What this looks like:**
- Responding to an incident that does not match a known playbook by applying the
  underlying incident response principles
- Developing a custom detection rule for a novel technique not yet in public rule sets
- Designing a security architecture for a non-standard environment using SABSA methodology

**How the degree develops this:** Assessment tasks deliberately include scenarios
without prescribed "correct" answers. The capstone requires synthesis across multiple
units to address a novel challenge.

## Graduate Profile by Pathway

### Operations Graduate

An operations graduate is a technical cybersecurity practitioner who can:
- Execute complex hands-on cybersecurity work in their chosen specialisation
- Map their actions to NICE DCWF tasks and ATT&CK techniques
- Produce professional-quality technical documentation
- Operate within Australian legal and ethical constraints
- Demonstrate their capabilities with a portfolio of evidence

### Strategy Graduate

A strategy graduate is a cybersecurity professional who can:
- Design, manage, and communicate cybersecurity programs at an organisational level
- Translate technical risk into business language
- Navigate Australian regulatory obligations across their sector
- Lead or contribute to governance, risk, and compliance programs
- Present credibly to executive and board audiences
