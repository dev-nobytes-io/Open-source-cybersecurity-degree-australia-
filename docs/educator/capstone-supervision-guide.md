# Capstone Supervision Guide

The capstone project is the integrative pinnacle of the degree. It requires
students to synthesise learning across all three layers into a substantial,
professional-quality deliverable appropriate to their chosen major.

This guide is for supervisors, facilitators, and institutional delivery partners
overseeing capstone projects.

---

## 1. Capstone Purpose and Design

The capstone serves three purposes:

1. **Integration** — Students draw on knowledge and skills from Foundation, Core,
   and Major units to address a complex, realistic challenge
2. **Portfolio** — The capstone produces a professional artefact that demonstrates
   graduate-level capability to employers
3. **Synthesis** — Students operate at Bloom's Levels 5–6 (Evaluate, Create),
   demonstrating the highest cognitive demand in the degree

The capstone is worth 24 CP — equivalent to 3 standard units — reflecting the
depth and scope of work required.

---

## 2. Capstone Scope by Major

Each major has a defined capstone scope. Supervisors must ensure the project
stays within scope and meets the minimum requirements.

### Operational Majors

| Major | Capstone Scope | Key Deliverables |
|---|---|---|
| **Threat Hunting** | Design and execute a full threat hunting engagement on a simulated enterprise environment | Hunt hypothesis library, hunting playbook, SIEM queries/rules, findings report, ATT&CK coverage map |
| **DFIR** | Conduct a complete forensic investigation of a simulated incident scenario | Chain of custody documentation, forensic timeline, evidence analysis report, incident narrative, recommendations |
| **CTI** | Produce a finished intelligence product on a specified threat actor or campaign | Threat actor profile (ATT&CK mapped), finished intelligence report (tactical + strategic), STIX bundle, dissemination plan |
| **Detection Engineering** | Design, build, test, and document a detection library for a specified threat category | 10+ detection rules (Sigma), test plan and results, ATT&CK coverage heatmap, tuning documentation, deployment guide |
| **CTE** | Plan and execute an adversary emulation exercise against a defined target environment | Emulation plan (ATT&CK-mapped), execution notes, purple team findings, remediation recommendations, TIBER-AU-aligned report |

### Strategic Majors

| Major | Capstone Scope | Key Deliverables |
|---|---|---|
| **Security Engineering** | Design a security architecture for a specified organisation type and threat model | Architecture diagrams, threat model, control mapping (NIST SP 800-53/ASD E8), implementation roadmap, design rationale |
| **Leadership & CISO** | Develop a 3-year cybersecurity strategy for a simulated Australian organisation | Strategy document, executive dashboard, board briefing (10 slides), breach scenario response plan, metrics framework |
| **GRC** | Conduct a comprehensive compliance assessment for a simulated Australian organisation | Gap analysis report (ISO 27001 + ASD E8), risk register, policy framework, audit plan, regulatory obligation mapping |

## 3. Supervision Model

### Supervision Load

One supervisor can effectively supervise 5–8 capstone students simultaneously
in a structured cohort context. For self-directed learners, community mentors
may provide lighter-touch supervision.

### Supervision Phases

**Phase 1 — Scoping (Weeks 1–2)**
- Student submits a 1-page capstone proposal specifying: major, scenario/environment,
  proposed deliverables, and timeline
- Supervisor reviews and approves or requests revision
- Key questions to resolve: Is the scope achievable? Is the scenario realistic?
  Are the deliverables appropriate to the major?

**Phase 2 — Development (Weeks 3–10)**
- Student works independently on the project
- Supervisor holds weekly or fortnightly check-ins (30 minutes)
- Check-in agenda: progress against milestones, blockers, quality concerns
- Supervisor provides guidance on approach but does not complete work for the student

**Phase 3 — Milestone Review (Weeks 4 and 8)**
- Student submits a work-in-progress draft at two milestones
- Supervisor provides written feedback within 5 working days
- Feedback focuses on: scope creep, accuracy, completeness, professional quality

**Phase 4 — Finalisation (Weeks 11–12)**
- Student completes and polishes all deliverables
- Final submission assembled as a portfolio package

**Phase 5 — Assessment (Week 13)**
- Supervisor marks final submission against rubric
- Optional: brief oral debrief (15–20 minutes) where student discusses their work
  and key decisions — recommended for facilitated delivery

## 4. Capstone Assessment Rubric

All capstones are assessed against these criteria regardless of major:

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Technical accuracy** | All technical content is accurate; no factual errors; demonstrates expert understanding | Content is accurate with minor gaps; demonstrates solid understanding | Some technical inaccuracies; understanding is partially demonstrated | Significant technical errors; understanding is not demonstrated |
| **Framework application** | Frameworks applied with sophistication; mappings are precise and well-justified | Frameworks applied correctly; mappings are appropriate | Frameworks referenced but not consistently or correctly applied | Frameworks not applied or applied incorrectly |
| **Australian context** | Australian context is integral to the work; demonstrates deep regulatory literacy | Australian context is included and accurate | Australian context is mentioned but superficial or partially accurate | Australian context is absent or inaccurate |
| **Professional quality** | Work meets or exceeds professional industry standards; ready for actual use | Work is professionally presented and clearly structured | Work is functional but has notable quality or presentation gaps | Work does not meet professional standards |
| **Synthesis and integration** | Demonstrates clear integration of knowledge across Foundation, Core, and Major; connections are explicit | Integration across layers is evident with some explicit connections | Some integration evident but largely confined to Major units | Work does not demonstrate integration across degree layers |
| **Evidence and reasoning** | All conclusions are supported by specific, well-documented evidence; reasoning is transparent | Most conclusions are supported by evidence; reasoning is generally clear | Some conclusions lack evidence; reasoning has gaps | Conclusions are unsupported; reasoning is absent or unclear |

## 5. Common Supervision Issues

### Scope Creep
The student attempts to do too much and produces shallow work across a wide scope
rather than deep work within a realistic scope.
**Response:** Return to the approved proposal; ask the student which deliverables
are most important and focus there.

### Superficial Framework Application
The student lists frameworks without applying them — e.g., mentioning ATT&CK
without mapping specific techniques.
**Response:** Ask the student to select three specific framework elements and
demonstrate them properly in the work, rather than listing all of them superficially.

### Fabricated or Incomplete Lab Work
The student reports results they did not actually achieve.
**Response:** Ask the student to walk through their process in the debrief.
A student who cannot explain their own results has not done the work.

### Inability to Complete Within Scope
Life circumstances, technical failures, or poor time management prevent completion.
**Response:** Delivery partners set their own extension policies. For self-directed
learners, the timeline is flexible — encourage the student to submit what is complete
and plan to finish remaining deliverables within a defined additional period.

## 6. Capstone and the Competency Profile

The capstone is the most significant single entry in a student's competency profile.
Students should complete the capstone section of `templates/competency-profile-template.md`
as a final step, linking to their capstone repository or report.

Supervisors should confirm that the student's NICE DCWF T-code and work role claims
in their competency profile are consistent with what the capstone demonstrates.
