# Assessment Moderation Guide

Assessment moderation ensures that judgements about student work are consistent,
fair, and defensible across different markers, cohorts, and delivery contexts.

This guide is for facilitators, delivery partners, and any context where more
than one person is marking student assessments.

---

## 1. Why Moderation Matters

Without moderation, the same piece of student work may receive different grades
from different markers. This creates inequity for students and undermines the
credibility of the qualification.

In a practitioner-led degree where content is authored by different experts and
delivered in multiple contexts, moderation is the mechanism that maintains
a consistent standard of graduate capability.

---

## 2. Types of Moderation

### Pre-Marking Moderation (Calibration)

Before marking begins for a summative assessment, all markers for that cohort
review the same set of sample responses and independently apply the rubric.
Markers then compare results and discuss discrepancies before marking the full cohort.

**When:** Before marking any summative assessment in a facilitated delivery context.

**Process:**
1. Facilitator selects 3–5 sample responses (one per performance level; use samples
   from a previous cohort or purpose-written exemplars if first delivery)
2. All markers independently apply the rubric to each sample
3. Markers compare scores — any discrepancy of more than one performance level
   on any criterion must be discussed and resolved
4. Agreement on boundary cases is documented before full marking begins

### Post-Marking Moderation (Review)

After marking is complete but before results are released to students, a sample
of marked work is reviewed by a second marker.

**Sample size:** Minimum 10% of submissions, or 5 submissions, whichever is greater.
Include: the highest-scoring submission, the lowest-scoring submission, at least
one borderline case, and a random sample of the remainder.

**Process:**
1. Second marker applies rubric independently to sampled submissions
2. Compare first and second marker scores
3. Discrepancies: if scores differ by one performance level on a criterion, use
   the first marker's score but note the discrepancy. If scores differ by two or
   more levels on any criterion, the submission must be discussed and re-marked.
4. Document moderation outcomes

### External Moderation

For delivery partners seeking institutional recognition or TEQSA compliance,
an external moderator (a practitioner not involved in delivery) should review
a sample of assessed work at least annually.

The external moderator reviews:
- Whether the rubric criteria are appropriate for the unit's learning outcomes
- Whether markers are applying the rubric consistently
- Whether the standard of "Proficient" performance is appropriate for AQF Level 7

## 3. Rubric Application Standards

### The Four Performance Levels

| Level | Meaning | When to Apply |
|---|---|---|
| **Exemplary** | Exceeds expectations in this criterion | Work demonstrates insight, precision, or sophistication beyond what the criterion requires |
| **Proficient** | Meets expectations fully | Work clearly satisfies the criterion at the standard expected |
| **Developing** | Partially meets expectations | Work addresses the criterion but with identifiable gaps, errors, or underdevelopment |
| **Beginning** | Does not yet meet expectations | Work does not adequately address the criterion |

### Borderline Cases

When a submission sits on the boundary between two levels:
- Default to the lower level
- Provide specific written feedback identifying what would achieve the higher level
- Do not split levels (there is no "Proficient–Exemplary" — choose one)

### Common Marking Errors

**Halo effect:** Marking all criteria highly because the overall impression is positive.
Apply each criterion independently.

**Central tendency:** Avoiding Exemplary and Beginning, giving most work Proficient
or Developing. Use the full range of levels — Exemplary work exists, as does
work that genuinely does not meet the standard.

**Leniency bias:** Inflating grades to avoid student disappointment. Beginning is
a legitimate grade for work that does not meet the standard, and honest feedback
at this level is more valuable to the student than an unearned Proficient.

**Penalising format over substance:** Mark the criterion as written. If a student
meets the criterion but uses an unexpected format or structure, this should not
reduce their score unless the criterion specifically requires a format.

## 4. Feedback Standards

### Required Elements in Summative Feedback

All summative feedback must include:
- A score for each rubric criterion (Exemplary / Proficient / Developing / Beginning)
- At least one specific, evidence-referenced comment per criterion
- For any Developing or Beginning criterion: a clear statement of what is missing
  or incorrect and what the student would need to do to achieve Proficient
- Overall comments noting strengths and the single most important improvement area

### What Feedback Should Not Include

- Vague comments ("Good work", "Needs improvement") without specifics
- Corrections of every minor issue — focus on the most significant
- Personal comments about the student
- Comments that compare the student to peers
- Grade changes or hints about resubmission without a formal policy basis

### Feedback Turnaround

- **Formative assessment:** 5 working days
- **Summative assessment:** 10 working days
- **Resubmissions (where permitted):** 5 working days from resubmission

---

## 5. Assessment Records

### What to Keep

Maintain the following for a minimum of 12 months after the assessment period:
- All marked submissions (digital copies)
- The rubric version used for marking
- Moderation records (calibration notes, post-marking moderation sample results)
- Any feedback provided to students

### Confidentiality

Student assessment records are confidential. Do not share individual student
work or grades with other students. Anonymised examples may be used for future
calibration exercises with student consent.

---

## 6. Resubmission Policy

Delivery partners set their own resubmission policies. The default recommendation:

- **Formative assessments:** May be resubmitted once, after feedback is received.
  No grade penalty.
- **Summative assessments:** Resubmission permitted once for submissions graded
  majority Developing or below. Resubmission addresses only the deficient criteria —
  students may not revise Proficient or Exemplary sections.
- **Late submissions:** Delivery partners set their own late submission policy.
  The degree does not prescribe penalties as pacing is flexible.

---

## 7. Appeals Process

Where a student disputes their summative assessment grade:
1. Student raises concern in writing with specific reference to the rubric and their submission
2. Marker reviews the disputed criteria and either upholds or revises their assessment
3. If unresolved, a second independent marker reviews only the disputed criteria
4. Second marker's judgement is final

Document all appeals and outcomes. Systematic appeals on the same criterion across
multiple students may indicate a rubric or marking calibration issue — escalate to
the Maintainer group via GitHub Issue.
