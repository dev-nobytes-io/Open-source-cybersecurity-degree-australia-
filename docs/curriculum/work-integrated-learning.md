# Work-Integrated Learning Framework

Work-Integrated Learning (WIL) connects formal education to professional practice
by incorporating structured workplace learning into the degree. This document defines
how WIL components can be incorporated into the Open-Source Cybersecurity Degree.

---

## 1. WIL in This Degree

### Current Position

The degree is primarily designed for self-directed, online learning. WIL components
are optional enhancements — they are not required to complete the degree.

For delivery partners (universities, employers), WIL components are strongly
recommended as they significantly increase student employability outcomes and
provide authentic assessment contexts.

### Why WIL Matters for Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is a practice discipline. The gap between classroom capability
and workplace readiness is real and can be bridged through:
- Exposure to production environments and real data (appropriately managed)
- Working alongside experienced practitioners
- Encountering problems that don't fit textbook scenarios
- Building professional networks before graduation

## 2. WIL Models

### Model A — Industry Mentorship (Recommended for all students)

**Description:** A practitioner mentor meets with the student regularly throughout
a unit or layer to provide guidance, real-world context, and feedback.

**Time commitment:** 1–2 hours per fortnight for the duration of a unit (~10–14 hours)

**Structure:**
- Student and mentor agree on focus areas at the start of the engagement
- Mentor reviews the student's lab work and provides practitioner feedback
- Mentor shares real-world examples and context relevant to the unit
- Student documents insights from mentoring sessions in their portfolio

**How to find a mentor:** Through the community mentorship program (GitHub Discussions),
AISA mentoring programs, or CIISec mentorship network.

**Assessment integration:** Mentorship is not directly assessed, but the insights
gained should be reflected in the student's summative work.

---

### Model B — Employer-Sponsored Cohort

**Description:** An employer funds a cohort of their staff through the degree
(or selected units) with structured workplace application tasks alongside each unit.

**Structure:**
- Staff study unit content in parallel with a work project that applies the
  unit's skills to the employer's actual environment
- Example: OC02 (Security Monitoring & SIEM) cohort run alongside a SIEM
  implementation or improvement project at the employer
- Assessment tasks may use anonymised real organisational data with employer consent

**WIL-enhanced assessment:** Employers may agree to allow students to use real
(anonymised) organisational artefacts in their assessments — threat models of
actual systems, risk registers based on real assets, incident reports from
actual events.

**Safeguards:** All real organisational data must be anonymised. Students must
follow their employer's confidentiality and data handling policies. The unit's
academic integrity policy still applies.

---

### Model C — Industry Project (Major / Capstone)

**Description:** Students complete their Major capstone project in partnership
with an industry host organisation, addressing a real challenge.

**Structure:**
- Industry host defines a project brief (e.g., design a threat hunting program,
  conduct a compliance gap assessment, build a detection library for a specific threat)
- Student completes the project as their capstone deliverable
- Industry host provides access to relevant (appropriately controlled) data,
  systems, or documentation
- Industry host provides a final feedback statement

**Assessment:** The standard capstone rubric applies. The industry context is
an enhancement, not a different assessment standard.

**Legal and confidentiality requirements:**
- A confidentiality/NDA agreement between the student and host organisation
  is required before project commencement
- All deliverables containing organisational data must be approved for release
  by the host before being submitted or published in the student's portfolio
- Delivery partners facilitating industry projects should use a standard project
  agreement template (contact Maintainers for template)

---

### Model D — Practitioner Guest Program

**Description:** Working practitioners contribute guest sessions to units,
providing real-world context and mentorship at scale.

**Structure:**
- Each major unit includes at least one 60-minute guest session from a practitioner
  currently working in the domain (see `docs/educator/facilitator-guide.md`)
- Practitioners are sourced through the IAB, community networks, and AISA/CIISec

**This is the lowest-effort WIL model and should be the baseline for all
facilitated delivery.**

---

## 3. WIL Quality Standards

Regardless of model, WIL components must:
- Not compromise the student's learning objectives — the work experience enhances,
  not replaces, the academic content
- Follow the academic integrity policy — work done in a WIL context is still the
  student's own work
- Be documented — students must record what they did, what they learned, and how
  it connects to the unit's learning outcomes
- Not expose students to unreasonable risk — all cybersecurity work performed in
  a WIL context must be properly authorised

---

## 4. WIL and the TEQSA HESF

TEQSA's Higher Education Standards Framework recognises WIL as a component of
quality learning environments (HESF Domain 2). Delivery partners seeking TEQSA
compliance should document their WIL arrangements and outcomes as part of their
quality assurance records.

The student's competency profile serves as the primary WIL learning record —
T-codes and SFIA skills demonstrated in WIL contexts should be documented with
appropriate evidence (as permitted by confidentiality agreements).
