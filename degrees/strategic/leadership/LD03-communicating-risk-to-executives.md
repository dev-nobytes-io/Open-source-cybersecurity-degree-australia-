## Overview

This unit teaches the single skill that most determines a security leader's success:
making cyber risk legible and actionable for boards and executives. Students learn
to translate technical reality into business-risk language, design board-grade risk
dashboards and reporting, and communicate uncertainty honestly so that
decision-makers can govern risk and allocate capital. It is the communication
counterpart to the program-and-risk emphasis of LD01–LD02: a well-managed risk
program is only as effective as the board's understanding of it.

In the Australian context, risk communication is shaped by APRA board-reporting
expectations and the ASX Corporate Governance Principles' cyber-risk disclosure
guidance. LD03 builds on SC06 (Stakeholder Communication), SC01 (Risk), and LD01–
LD02, and feeds LD05 (crisis comms) and the LD06 capstone.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

1. **Analyse** an executive/board audience to frame cyber risk in business terms.
2. **Synthesise** technical findings into a board-level risk narrative.
3. **Design** a CISO risk dashboard with decision-driving metrics.
4. **Evaluate** how to communicate uncertainty and confidence honestly to a board.
5. **Analyse** Australian board-reporting and disclosure expectations for cyber
   risk.
6. **Create** board-grade risk communications that drive decisions.

## Topics

### Topic 1: The Board's View of Cyber Risk

This topic teaches how boards think: in terms of enterprise risk, fiduciary duty,
materiality, and capital allocation — not controls or CVEs. It frames cyber as one
risk among many competing for board attention.

**Key concepts:**
- Enterprise risk and fiduciary duty
- Materiality and capital allocation
- Cyber as one board risk among many

---

### Topic 2: Translating Technical Risk to Business Risk

This topic teaches the core translation: from technical findings to business impact
(financial, operational, reputational, regulatory), leading with the "so what", and
expressing risk in terms tied to decisions.

**Key concepts:**
- Technical finding → business impact
- Leading with the "so what"
- Decision-linked risk statements

---

### Topic 3: Risk Dashboards and Reporting

This topic teaches designing board/executive reporting: what to show, how often, and
how to choose metrics (KRIs) that inform governance without drowning the board in
detail — and visualising risk honestly.

**Key concepts:**
- Dashboard design and metric selection (KRIs)
- Reporting cadence and depth by audience
- Honest risk visualisation

---

### Topic 4: Communicating Uncertainty

Cyber risk is uncertain; pretending otherwise destroys credibility. This topic
teaches conveying likelihood, confidence, and ranges honestly, avoiding both false
precision and vague hand-waving.

**Key concepts:**
- Likelihood, confidence, and ranges
- Avoiding false precision and vagueness
- Credibility through honesty

---

### Topic 5: Australian Board Reporting and Disclosure

This topic covers the Australian expectations: APRA board-reporting expectations for
regulated entities, the ASX Corporate Governance Principles on cyber-risk oversight
and disclosure, and the materiality/continuous-disclosure considerations for listed
entities.

**Key concepts:**
- APRA board-reporting expectations
- ASX Corporate Governance Principles (cyber)
- Materiality and continuous disclosure

**Australian context:** This topic is the Australian board-reporting/disclosure
frame.

---

### Topic 6: Influence and the Risk Conversation

This topic teaches the CISO as an influencer in the boardroom: building trust over
time, handling difficult questions, aligning with other risk functions, and turning
the risk conversation into supported decisions and funding.

**Key concepts:**
- Building board trust over time
- Handling challenge in the boardroom
- Turning risk conversations into decisions

## Labs & Exercises

### Lab 1: Board Risk Brief (deliverable activity)

**Objective:** Convert a technical incident report into a one-page board-level risk
brief.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 1, 2, and 4

**Environment:**
- No technical environment — a communication deliverable. Any device within the
  8 GB / 4-core / 50 GB spec.

**Instructions:**

1. Take a provided technical incident/assessment report.
2. Identify the business impacts (financial, operational, reputational, regulatory).
3. Write a one-page board brief leading with the business risk and the decision
   sought.
4. Express likelihood and confidence honestly (no false precision).
5. Include a clear recommendation and what the board must decide.
6. Remove all jargon while preserving accuracy.

**Expected Output:**

A one-page board risk brief leading with business impact, with honest uncertainty,
a clear recommendation, and no jargon. Learners can state the core message in one
sentence.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. What did you cut, and why was it not board-relevant?
2. How did you convey uncertainty without undermining the recommendation?
3. Which impact dimension most moved the board, and why?

---

### Lab 2: CISO Dashboard (design activity)

**Objective:** Design a board/executive risk dashboard with decision-driving
metrics.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 3, 5, and 6 and Lab 1

**Environment:**
- No technical environment — a design deliverable (mock dashboard). Any device.

**Instructions:**

1. Take a fictional Australian organisation and its board's concerns.
2. Choose 5–7 metrics (KRIs) that inform governance and risk decisions.
3. Justify each metric (what decision it supports) and reject two vanity metrics.
4. Design the dashboard layout, leading with the most decision-relevant view.
5. Define cadence and how exceptions are escalated.
6. Align the dashboard to APRA/ASX board-reporting expectations.

**Expected Output:**

A board dashboard design with justified, non-vanity metrics, cadence, escalation,
and alignment to Australian reporting expectations. Learners can defend each metric
against a "so what" test.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. Which metric best informs a board decision, and which vanity metric did you
   reject?
2. How does the dashboard support APRA/ASX reporting expectations?
3. How would the dashboard change during an active incident?

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment: Translate-to-Board Drill

**Type:** Short exercise with answer key

**Description:** Given technical findings, students rewrite each as a board-level
risk statement leading with business impact. Self-marked.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO1, LO2

**Feedback mechanism:** Answer key with model board-level rewrites.

---

### Summative Assessment: Executive Risk Communication Portfolio

**Type:** Communication portfolio

**Description:** For an assigned Australian organisation, students produce (a) a
board risk brief on a given scenario, (b) a CISO risk dashboard design with
justified metrics, (c) an analysis of the applicable APRA/ASX reporting and
disclosure expectations, and (d) a short reflection on communicating uncertainty.
Deliverable: a portfolio (~2,000–2,500 words across artefacts).

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO1, LO2, LO3, LO4, LO5, LO6

**Assessment-Learning Outcome Mapping:**

| Assessment Task | Learning Outcomes |
|---|---|
| Board risk brief | LO1, LO2, LO6 |
| CISO dashboard | LO3 |
| Reporting/disclosure analysis | LO5 |
| Uncertainty reflection | LO4 |

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Audience framing | Precisely board-pitched | Mostly | Partial | Poor |
| Risk translation | Clear business-impact narrative | Sound | Mixed | Technical/unclear |
| Dashboard & metrics | Decision-driving, non-vanity | Adequate | Some vanity | Poor |
| Uncertainty & honesty | Honest, credible | Reasonable | Vague/overconfident | Misleading |
| Regulatory alignment | Accurate APRA/ASX alignment | Mostly | Superficial | Absent |
