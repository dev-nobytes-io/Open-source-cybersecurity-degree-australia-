## Overview

This unit is the program-management heart of the Leadership major. Students learn to
build a security strategy and a multi-year roadmap: assessing current maturity,
setting a risk-prioritised target, sequencing initiatives under real constraints,
and making the investment case in terms the board will fund. It treats the security
program as a managed portfolio driven by risk — the discipline that turns a CISO
mandate (LD01) into delivered capability and measurable risk reduction.

Strategy and roadmapping are where security program management and risk converge:
every roadmap item exists to reduce a prioritised risk or unlock a business
outcome, and every dollar is justified against exposure (building on SC01) and
commercial enablement (building on SC03/SC05). In the Australian context, the ASD
Essential Eight Maturity Model is a common roadmap anchor, and APRA-regulated
entities must demonstrate risk-driven investment. LD02 builds on SC05, SC01, and
LD01, and feeds LD03 and the LD06 capstone.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

1. **Evaluate** an organisation's security maturity using a recognised model.
2. **Synthesise** a risk-prioritised, multi-year security strategy and roadmap.
3. **Design** a risk-adjusted investment case for security initiatives.
4. **Analyse** how to sequence initiatives under budget, dependency, and capability
   constraints.
5. **Evaluate** security metrics that demonstrate program progress and risk
   reduction.
6. **Recommend** a strategy and roadmap defensible to a board.

## Topics

### Topic 1: From Risk to Strategy

This topic establishes that security strategy flows from risk: the organisation's
risk profile, appetite, and business objectives define what the program must
achieve. It connects SC01's risk outputs to strategic priorities.

**Key concepts:**
- Risk profile and appetite as strategy inputs
- Aligning security strategy to business objectives
- Strategy as prioritised risk reduction

---

### Topic 2: Maturity Assessment

This topic teaches assessing current capability with maturity models — NIST CSF 2.0
tiers/profiles, the ASD Essential Eight Maturity Model, and CMMI-style scales — to
establish a credible baseline and a realistic target.

**Key concepts:**
- NIST CSF 2.0 tiers/profiles and Essential Eight Maturity Model
- Baseline vs realistic target maturity
- Honest, evidence-based assessment

**Australian context:** The Essential Eight Maturity Model is a common Australian
roadmap anchor.

---

### Topic 3: Building the Roadmap

This topic teaches constructing a multi-year roadmap: deriving initiatives from the
maturity gap and risk register, sequencing by risk reduction, dependency, effort,
and value, and phasing toward defined milestones (including compliance/commercial
milestones from SC03).

**Key concepts:**
- Initiative derivation from gaps and risk
- Sequencing and phasing
- Milestones (risk, compliance, commercial)

---

### Topic 4: The Investment Case

This topic teaches making the funding argument: risk-adjusted cost-benefit, tying
spend to quantified exposure reduction (SC01) and to enablement (SC03), and framing
options (do-nothing/minimum/recommended) for a board decision.

**Key concepts:**
- Risk-adjusted cost-benefit
- Spend → exposure reduction and enablement
- Optioned proposals for board decisions

---

### Topic 5: Strategy Metrics

This topic teaches the metrics that show a strategy is working: leading vs lagging,
risk-reduction and maturity-progress indicators, and avoiding vanity metrics —
metrics a board will trust and act on (links to LD03).

**Key concepts:**
- Leading/lagging, risk-reduction, maturity-progress metrics
- Avoiding vanity metrics
- Board-trustworthy measurement

---

### Topic 6: Defending the Strategy

This topic teaches presenting and defending a strategy: the executive narrative,
handling challenge and trade-off questions, and adapting the strategy as risk and
business change — keeping it a living plan, not a shelf document.

**Key concepts:**
- The executive strategy narrative
- Handling challenge and trade-offs
- Keeping strategy adaptive

## Labs & Exercises

### Lab 1: 3-Year Roadmap (deliverable activity)

**Objective:** Build a risk-prioritised three-year security roadmap for a fictional
organisation using a maturity model.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 1–3 and 5

**Environment:**
- No technical environment — a strategy deliverable using NIST CSF 2.0 / Essential
  Eight Maturity Model references. Any device within the 8 GB / 4-core / 50 GB spec.

**Instructions:**

1. Take a fictional Australian organisation with a described risk profile.
2. Assess current maturity against the Essential Eight Maturity Model and NIST CSF
   tiers; set a realistic target.
3. Derive the initiatives needed to close the gap, linked to prioritised risks.
4. Sequence them into a phased 3-year roadmap with milestones.
5. Define 4–6 metrics that show progress and risk reduction.
6. Note key dependencies and assumptions.

**Expected Output:**

A phased 3-year roadmap with maturity baseline/target, risk-linked initiatives,
milestones, and metrics. Learners can justify the sequencing against risk and
constraints.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. Why is a realistic target better than "maximum maturity everywhere"?
2. Which initiative gives the best risk reduction per dollar?
3. How would you adapt the roadmap if the budget were halved?

---

### Lab 2: Investment Case (deliverable activity)

**Objective:** Write a risk-adjusted business case for a security investment.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 4–6 and Lab 1

**Environment:**
- No technical environment — a business-case deliverable. Any device.

**Instructions:**

1. Select a key initiative from the Lab 1 roadmap.
2. Estimate its cost (capex/opex) and the exposure it reduces (link to SC01
   quantification).
3. Articulate any commercial enablement (markets/contracts/trust) it unlocks.
4. Present three options (do-nothing / minimum / recommended) with consequences.
5. Frame the recommendation in risk-adjusted, board-appropriate language.
6. State the metrics that would prove the investment delivered.

**Expected Output:**

A risk-adjusted investment case with costed options, exposure-reduction and
enablement arguments, and success metrics. Learners can defend the recommendation
to a non-technical board.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. Which argument — risk reduction or enablement — was more persuasive, and why?
2. How did presenting options change the board conversation versus a single ask?
3. What would make a board reject this case, and how did you pre-empt it?

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment: Roadmap Critique

**Type:** Peer/self review exercise with answer key

**Description:** Given a draft roadmap, students critique its risk-linkage,
sequencing, and metrics, and propose improvements. Self-marked.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO2, LO5

**Feedback mechanism:** Answer key with a model critique.

---

### Summative Assessment: Security Strategy & Investment Plan

**Type:** Executive strategy report

**Description:** For an assigned Australian organisation, students (a) assess
maturity, (b) synthesise a risk-prioritised 3-year strategy and roadmap, (c) build a
risk-adjusted investment case with options, (d) define progress/risk metrics, and
(e) present a board-defensible recommendation. Deliverable: 2,500–3,000 word
strategy with roadmap, investment case, and metrics.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO1, LO2, LO3, LO4, LO5, LO6

**Assessment-Learning Outcome Mapping:**

| Assessment Task | Learning Outcomes |
|---|---|
| Maturity assessment | LO1 |
| Strategy & roadmap | LO2, LO4 |
| Investment case | LO3 |
| Metrics & defence | LO5, LO6 |

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strategy & risk linkage | Every initiative risk-justified | Mostly linked | Partial | Weak |
| Roadmap | Realistic, sequenced, milestone-driven | Sound | Generic | Weak |
| Investment case | Compelling, risk-adjusted, optioned | Reasonable | Thin | Unjustified |
| Metrics | Decision-driving, non-vanity | Adequate | Some vanity | Poor |
| Communication | Board-ready, clear | Clear with minor lapses | Disorganised | Unclear |
