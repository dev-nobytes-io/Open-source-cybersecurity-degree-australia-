## Overview

A security program is delivered by people, and this unit teaches the CISO's
people-leadership discipline: designing a security team structure fit for the
organisation's size and risk, hiring against recognised competency frameworks,
building culture, defining career pathways, and retaining talent in a tight market.
It connects directly to the program-management emphasis of the major — resourcing
is one of the largest levers on what a program can actually deliver against risk.

In the Australian context, the persistent cyber-skills shortage is a real
constraint on every program, and ASD/government workforce initiatives shape the
talent landscape. Team design also draws on the workforce frameworks used across
this degree (NICE/DCWF, SFIA, ASD CSF) to define roles and competencies. LD04
builds on SC05 (Program Management) and LD01–LD02, and supports the LD06 capstone.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

1. **Design** a security team structure appropriate to an organisation's size and
   risk.
2. **Analyse** roles and competencies using workforce frameworks (NICE/DCWF, SFIA,
   ASD CSF).
3. **Evaluate** build-vs-buy-vs-MSSP resourcing decisions.
4. **Synthesise** a culture and career-pathway approach for a security team.
5. **Recommend** a talent-retention strategy for the Australian market.
6. **Evaluate** the people-risk and resourcing constraints on a program.

## Topics

### Topic 1: Designing the Security Function

This topic teaches structuring a security team: core functions (governance, ops/SOC,
engineering, GRC, IR), how structure scales with organisation size and risk, and the
trade-offs of centralised vs federated models.

**Key concepts:**
- Core security functions and their interfaces
- Scaling structure to size and risk
- Centralised vs federated team models

---

### Topic 2: Roles, Competencies, and Workforce Frameworks

This topic teaches defining roles and competencies using the frameworks woven
through this degree — NICE/DCWF work roles and KSATs, SFIA skills/levels, and the
ASD CSF — so hiring and development are framework-grounded.

**Key concepts:**
- NICE/DCWF work roles and KSATs
- SFIA skills and responsibility levels
- ASD CSF competencies

---

### Topic 3: Resourcing — Build, Buy, or MSSP

This topic evaluates resourcing models: in-house teams, outsourced/MSSP/MDR, and
hybrid — and how to decide based on risk, capability, cost, and control, including
the governance of outsourced security.

**Key concepts:**
- In-house vs MSSP/MDR vs hybrid
- Decision factors (risk, capability, cost, control)
- Governing outsourced security

---

### Topic 4: Culture and Leadership

This topic covers building a healthy, high-performing security culture: psychological
safety, blameless practices (linking to OC04/DFIR), diversity, and the leader's role
in setting tone and avoiding burnout.

**Key concepts:**
- Psychological safety and blameless culture
- Diversity and inclusion in security teams
- Leadership tone and burnout prevention

---

### Topic 5: Career Pathways and Development

This topic teaches designing career pathways: technical vs management tracks, skills
development mapped to frameworks, and using this degree's own structure as a
development map — keeping people growing and engaged.

**Key concepts:**
- Technical and management career tracks
- Framework-mapped development
- Growth as a retention lever

---

### Topic 6: Talent and the Australian Market

This topic addresses the Australian talent reality: the skills shortage, salary and
competition dynamics, ASD/government workforce initiatives, and concrete retention
strategies for a high-attrition field.

**Key concepts:**
- The Australian cyber-skills shortage
- Market and competition dynamics
- Retention strategies that work

**Australian context:** This topic is the Australian cyber workforce/talent
landscape.

## Labs & Exercises

### Lab 1: Team Structure Design (design activity)

**Objective:** Design a security team structure for a given organisation size and
risk profile.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 1–3

**Environment:**
- No technical environment — a design deliverable using NICE/DCWF, SFIA, and ASD
  CSF references. Any device within the 8 GB / 4-core / 50 GB spec.

**Instructions:**

1. Take an organisation profile (size, sector, risk).
2. Design a security team structure with named roles and reporting lines.
3. Map each role to NICE/DCWF work roles and SFIA skills/levels.
4. Decide which functions to keep in-house vs outsource (MSSP/MDR) and justify it.
5. Identify the two roles hardest to fill in the Australian market.
6. Note the people-risks and single points of failure in the design.

**Expected Output:**

A framework-mapped team structure with reporting lines, a build/buy decision, and an
analysis of hard-to-fill roles and people-risks. Learners can justify the structure
against the organisation's risk.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. How did organisation size and risk change the structure?
2. Which function did you outsource, and how would you govern it?
3. Which single point of failure most worries you, and why?

---

### Lab 2: Retention Strategy (deliverable activity)

**Objective:** Write a talent-retention strategy for a fictional Australian SOC team
facing high attrition.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 4–6 and Lab 1

**Environment:**
- No technical environment — a strategy deliverable. Any device.

**Instructions:**

1. Take a fictional Australian SOC team with described attrition drivers.
2. Diagnose the likely root causes (burnout, pay, growth, culture).
3. Propose a retention strategy across culture, career pathways, and conditions.
4. Map development pathways to workforce frameworks (and this degree).
5. Account for the Australian market (competition, salary, ASD initiatives).
6. Define how you would measure whether retention improved.

**Expected Output:**

A diagnosed retention strategy spanning culture, pathways, and conditions, with
market context and success metrics. Learners can connect retention to program
delivery and risk.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. Which retention lever is cheapest with the biggest effect, and why?
2. How does attrition translate into program and risk consequences?
3. How would the strategy differ for a regional vs metro Australian employer?
