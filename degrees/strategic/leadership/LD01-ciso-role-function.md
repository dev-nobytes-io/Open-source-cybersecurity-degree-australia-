## Overview

This unit examines the role and function of the Chief Information Security Officer
(CISO): the mandate, where security reports, how the CISO engages the board, and —
the throughline of this major — how the CISO owns and runs **security as a managed
program and a risk function**. Students learn that the CISO's core product is not
technology but *managed risk*: a defensible, prioritised, resourced program that an
organisation's leadership understands and backs. The unit establishes the
accountability, scope, and governance context in which every other leadership unit
operates.

In the Australian context the CISO role is increasingly shaped by regulation:
**APRA CPS 234** places explicit obligations on boards and senior management for
information-security risk, and ASX-listed entities face governance and disclosure
expectations. LD01 builds on SC03 (Governance), SC05 (Security Program Management),
and SC01 (Risk) and sets up LD02–LD06. Per the major's design, content should be
authored or reviewed by people with genuine CISO/Security Director experience.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

1. **Analyse** the CISO mandate and how reporting lines shape authority and
   independence.
2. **Evaluate** how the CISO owns security as a program and a risk function.
3. **Analyse** board and senior-management obligations for security risk in the
   Australian regulatory context (APRA CPS 234).
4. **Evaluate** CISO operating models (reporting lines, scope, virtual/fractional
   CISO) for a given organisation.
5. **Recommend** a CISO mandate and governance structure for an organisation.
6. **Design** a CISO mandate document that establishes program and risk
   accountability.

## Topics

### Topic 1: What a CISO Actually Does

This topic frames the CISO's real job: translating business risk into a managed
security program, owning the risk narrative to the board, and balancing protection
with enablement. It distinguishes the CISO from technical-lead roles and introduces
the "CISO as risk executive" framing that runs through the major.

**Key concepts:**
- The CISO as risk executive, not chief technologist
- Protection vs business enablement
- Owning the security program end-to-end

---

### Topic 2: Mandate, Authority, and Reporting Lines

Where the CISO reports shapes everything. This topic analyses common reporting
lines (to CIO, CTO, COO, CEO, Risk, or board), their effect on independence and
authority, and the conflicts of interest each can create — and what a strong
mandate must contain.

**Key concepts:**
- Reporting lines and their trade-offs
- Independence, authority, and conflicts of interest
- Components of a strong CISO mandate

---

### Topic 3: Security as a Program and Risk Function

This is the major's emphasis made explicit at LD01: the CISO runs security as a
*program* (governed, resourced, measured — building on SC05) anchored on *risk*
(prioritised, owned, communicated — building on SC01). This topic connects the
mandate to program ownership and risk accountability.

**Key concepts:**
- Program ownership (strategy, resourcing, measurement)
- Risk ownership, appetite, and acceptance authority
- Linking the mandate to program and risk accountability

---

### Topic 4: Board Engagement and Governance

This topic covers how the CISO engages the board and executive: the cadence and
content of board reporting, the board's oversight role, and committee structures
(risk/audit committees) — and how the CISO earns and keeps board confidence.

**Key concepts:**
- Board oversight and committee structures
- Reporting cadence and content
- Building board confidence

**Australian context:** APRA CPS 234 makes the board and senior management
accountable for information-security risk.

---

### Topic 5: The Australian Regulatory Frame for the CISO

This topic details the Australian obligations shaping the role: APRA CPS 234 board/
senior-management responsibilities, the broader operational-risk standard CPS 230,
and the governance/disclosure expectations on ASX-listed entities — and what they
mean for how a CISO must operate.

**Key concepts:**
- APRA CPS 234 (and CPS 230) leadership obligations
- ASX governance/disclosure expectations
- Implications for the CISO operating model

**Australian context:** This topic is the Australian regulatory frame for security
leadership.

---

### Topic 6: CISO Operating Models

This topic evaluates how the CISO function is structured: full-time vs virtual/
fractional CISO, centralised vs federated security, and scope boundaries (does the
CISO own privacy, resilience, fraud?) — matched to organisation size and risk.

**Key concepts:**
- Full-time vs virtual/fractional CISO
- Centralised vs federated models
- Scope boundaries and adjacencies

## Labs & Exercises

### Lab 1: Reporting-Structure Analysis (case-study activity)

**Objective:** Analyse the CISO/security reporting structures of real Australian
organisations and evaluate their implications.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 2, 4, and 5

**Environment:**
- No technical environment required — a case-study activity using public
  disclosures (annual reports, governance statements). Any device within the
  8 GB / 4-core / 50 GB spec.

**Instructions:**

1. Select three ASX-listed Australian companies (ideally across sectors).
2. From public disclosures, map where security/risk appears to report and how the
   board oversees it.
3. Identify structural differences and the independence/authority implications of
   each.
4. Relate each to APRA CPS 234 / ASX governance expectations where applicable.
5. Assess which structure best supports a risk-owning CISO and why.
6. Summarise the trade-offs.

**Expected Output:**

A comparison of three reporting structures with independence/authority analysis and
a reasoned judgement on which best supports a risk-owning CISO. Learners can justify
the assessment against governance expectations.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. Which reporting line most undermines CISO independence, and why?
2. How does APRA CPS 234 change board expectations of the CISO?
3. Which structure would you recommend for a regulated entity, and why?

---

### Lab 2: CISO Mandate (deliverable activity)

**Objective:** Draft a CISO mandate document establishing program and risk
accountability for a fictional Australian organisation.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 1–3 and 6

**Environment:**
- No technical environment — a document deliverable. Any device.

**Instructions:**

1. Take a fictional Australian bank (regulated by APRA).
2. Draft a CISO mandate covering: purpose, scope, authority, reporting line, and
   board-engagement cadence.
3. Define the CISO's program ownership (strategy, resourcing, measurement).
4. Define risk ownership: appetite alignment, acceptance authority, and escalation.
5. Reference the relevant APRA CPS 234 board/senior-management obligations.
6. State the decisions the CISO can make alone vs those requiring board approval.

**Expected Output:**

A complete, signable CISO mandate establishing program and risk accountability,
with authority boundaries and APRA-aligned governance. Learners can defend each
clause.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. Which authority was hardest to define, and why does ambiguity there cause
   problems?
2. How does the mandate make the CISO accountable for risk, not just controls?
3. What would you escalate to the board, and what would you own?

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment: Operating-Model Critique

**Type:** Short-answer exercise with answer key

**Description:** Given organisation profiles, students recommend a CISO operating
model and reporting line and justify it. Self-marked.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO4, LO5

**Feedback mechanism:** Answer key with a model recommendation and rationale per
profile.

---

### Summative Assessment: CISO Mandate & Governance Recommendation

**Type:** Executive report

**Description:** For an assigned Australian regulated organisation, students (a)
analyse the appropriate reporting line and operating model, (b) design a CISO
mandate establishing program and risk accountability, (c) map the board/senior-
management obligations under APRA CPS 234, and (d) recommend a governance structure
with justification. Deliverable: 2,000–2,500 word report with the mandate document.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO1, LO2, LO3, LO4, LO5, LO6

**Assessment-Learning Outcome Mapping:**

| Assessment Task | Learning Outcomes |
|---|---|
| Reporting line / operating model analysis | LO1, LO4 |
| CISO mandate (program + risk accountability) | LO2, LO6 |
| APRA CPS 234 obligation mapping | LO3 |
| Governance recommendation | LO5 |

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Role & governance insight | Sophisticated, accountability-focused | Sound | Partial | Weak |
| Program & risk framing | CISO clearly owns program and risk | Mostly clear | Superficial | Missing |
| Regulatory accuracy | Precise APRA CPS 234 mapping | Mostly correct | Partial | Incorrect |
| Recommendation | Well-justified, organisation-fit | Reasonable | Generic | Unjustified |
| Communication | Executive-ready, clear | Clear with minor lapses | Disorganised | Unclear |
