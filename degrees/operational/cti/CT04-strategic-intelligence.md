## Overview

This unit teaches strategic cyber threat intelligence: the analysis of geopolitical
context, sector-level threat trends, and long-horizon risk, produced for executive
and board audiences. Where technical intelligence (CT03) answers "what should we
block today", strategic intelligence answers "how is our threat environment
changing and what should we invest in". Students learn to analyse the forces
shaping the threat landscape and to communicate them to non-technical
decision-makers who allocate capital and set risk appetite.

Strategic intelligence is where CTI meets leadership and GRC. In the Australian
context, it must account for Australia's geopolitical position — the QUAD security
partnership, regional dynamics, and the sectoral risks to critical infrastructure
covered by the SOCI regime. CT04 builds on CT01–CT02 and connects to SC06
(Stakeholder Communication) and the strategic majors. It feeds CT06.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

1. **Analyse** the geopolitical and economic forces that shape the cyber threat
   landscape.
2. **Evaluate** sector-level threat trends relevant to an organisation's risk.
3. **Synthesise** multi-source reporting into strategic assessments with a forward
   view.
4. **Analyse** Australia's strategic context (regional dynamics, QUAD, critical
   infrastructure) for threat implications.
5. **Recommend** strategic implications and priorities to a non-technical executive
   audience.
6. **Create** a strategic intelligence briefing tailored to a CISO/board consumer.

## Topics

### Topic 1: What Strategic Intelligence Is

This topic distinguishes strategic from operational/technical intelligence by time
horizon, audience, and purpose: informing risk appetite, investment, and strategy
rather than immediate defensive action.

**Key concepts:**
- Strategic vs operational vs technical (horizon/audience/purpose)
- Informing risk and investment decisions
- The executive consumer

---

### Topic 2: Geopolitics and the Threat Landscape

State and state-aligned activity, economic motivation, and conflict shape cyber
threats. This topic teaches analysing geopolitical and economic drivers and
connecting them to changes in adversary behaviour and targeting.

**Key concepts:**
- State, criminal, and hacktivist motivations
- Geopolitical/economic drivers of cyber activity
- From geopolitics to targeting shifts

---

### Topic 3: Sector Threat Analysis

Threats are not uniform across sectors. This topic teaches profiling the threat
landscape for a sector (e.g. finance, health, critical infrastructure): who targets
it, why, with what TTPs, and how that is trending.

**Key concepts:**
- Sector-specific threat profiles
- Trend analysis over time
- Relating sector threats to an organisation

**Australian context:** Australian critical-infrastructure sectors and their SOCI
obligations frame the sector analysis.

---

### Topic 4: Australia's Strategic Context

This topic covers Australia's specific strategic position: regional dynamics, the
QUAD partnership, alliance relationships, and the implications for the cyber threats
facing Australian organisations and government.

**Key concepts:**
- Australia's regional/alliance context (incl. QUAD)
- Implications for Australian targeting
- Critical-infrastructure strategic risk

**Australian context:** This topic is Australia's strategic environment analysed
for cyber implications.

---

### Topic 5: Forecasting and Estimative Analysis

Strategic intelligence looks forward. This topic teaches disciplined forecasting:
scenario analysis, indicators-and-warning, and the honest use of estimative
language and confidence over uncertain horizons.

**Key concepts:**
- Scenario analysis and indicators & warning
- Estimative language for forecasts
- Communicating uncertainty over time

---

### Topic 6: Briefing the Executive

This topic teaches producing and delivering strategic products: the executive
briefing format, leading with "so what", translating threat into business risk and
investment implications, and tailoring to a board's decisions (linking to SC06).

**Key concepts:**
- Executive briefing structure; lead with implications
- Threat → business risk → investment
- Tailoring to board decisions

**Australian context:** Briefings reference Australian sector and regulatory
realities (e.g. SOCI, sector advisories).

## Labs & Exercises

### Lab 1: Sector Threat Assessment

**Objective:** Produce a strategic threat assessment for an Australian sector.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 1–4

**Environment:**
- Operating System: any (analysis lab)
- Tools: open reporting, ASD Annual Cyber Threat Report, sector advisories (free)
- Minimum hardware: trivial; within the 8 GB / 4-core / 50 GB spec

**Instructions:**

1. Choose an Australian sector (e.g. energy, health, finance).
2. Synthesise multi-source reporting on who targets it and why.
3. Analyse the trend (improving/worsening) and the key drivers (incl. geopolitical).
4. Relate the sector threat to a representative organisation's risk.
5. State a forward view with estimative language and confidence.
6. Summarise the strategic implications.

**Expected Output:**

A sector threat assessment with a sourced threat picture, trend analysis, drivers,
a forward view with confidence, and strategic implications. Learners can defend the
forward view's caveats.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. Which geopolitical driver most shapes this sector's threat, and why?
2. How confident is your forward view, and what would change it?
3. How do SOCI obligations interact with this sector's strategic risk?

---

### Lab 2: Strategic Briefing for a CISO

**Objective:** Produce a one-page strategic intelligence briefing for a
non-technical CISO/board audience.

**Prerequisites:**
- Topics 5–6 and Lab 1

**Environment:**
- Operating System: any
- Tools: a briefing template
- Minimum hardware: trivial

**Instructions:**

1. Take the Lab 1 assessment and a defined CISO/board decision context.
2. Distil it to a one-page briefing leading with the business implication.
3. Translate threat into risk and investment implications (no jargon).
4. Include a clear, prioritised "so what / recommended focus".
5. State confidence and key assumptions plainly.
6. Anticipate two board questions and prepare concise answers.

**Expected Output:**

A one-page executive briefing leading with implications, jargon-free, with
prioritised recommendations and stated confidence. Learners can deliver the core
message in one sentence.

**Reflection Questions:**

1. What did you cut to make it board-appropriate, and why?
2. How did you express threat without technical language?
3. Which recommendation best ties to an investment decision?

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment: Translate to Strategic

**Type:** Short exercise with answer key

**Description:** Given technical/operational findings, students rewrite them as
strategic implications for an executive. Self-marked.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO5, LO6

**Feedback mechanism:** Answer key with model strategic rewrites.

---

### Summative Assessment: Strategic Intelligence Product

**Type:** Analytical briefing

**Description:** For an assigned Australian organisation/sector, students (a)
analyse the geopolitical and sector drivers, (b) synthesise a forward-looking
strategic assessment, (c) analyse Australia's strategic context implications, and
(d) deliver an executive briefing with prioritised, investment-relevant
recommendations and stated confidence. Deliverable: a strategic assessment
(~2,000 words) plus a one-page executive briefing.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed:** LO1, LO2, LO3, LO4, LO5, LO6

**Assessment-Learning Outcome Mapping:**

| Assessment Task | Learning Outcomes |
|---|---|
| Geopolitical/sector analysis | LO1, LO2 |
| Strategic synthesis & forecast | LO3 |
| Australian context | LO4 |
| Executive briefing | LO5, LO6 |

**Rubric:**

| Criterion | Exemplary | Proficient | Developing | Beginning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic analysis | Insightful, well-sourced, forward-looking | Sound | Partial | Weak |
| Australian context | Nuanced, relevant | Adequate | Superficial | Absent |
| Forecasting | Disciplined, honest about uncertainty | Reasonable | Vague | Overconfident |
| Executive communication | Compelling, jargon-free, decision-driving | Clear | Mixed | Unclear |
| Recommendations | Prioritised, investment-relevant | Reasonable | Generic | Absent |
